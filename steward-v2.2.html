Safeguards and controls

Use the strongest feasible control. Track ownership, verification, dependencies, residual burdens, and what happens when a safeguard fails.

Safeguard hierarchy

1EliminateRemove the hazard, burden, collection, or unethical use entirely.Strongest
2SubstituteUse a less harmful approach, material, process, or data practice.Strong
3RedesignChange the architecture or workflow so the burden is structurally reduced.Preferred
4Technical controlInterlocks, isolation, minimization, encryption, automation, and enforced limits.Technical
5Procedural controlRules, approvals, audits, retention schedules, and operating procedures.Administrative
6Train or warnNotices, training, labels, disclosures, and instructions.Weaker
7Individual behaviorDepend on vigilance, memory, personal protective action, or good intentions.Weakest

Patterns and reasoning checks

Use contextual patterns to ask better questions, then examine common ways ethical reasoning becomes distorted, incomplete, or too convenient.

Prompts and signals, not moral automation. Patterns do not supply conclusions. Failure-mode signals are heuristic review prompts, not findings, violations, or scores. A human reviewer must investigate and document each disposition.

Start with questions, not the answer.

STEWARD is a structured engineering ethics record. It helps you preserve evidence, assumptions, burdens, disagreement, and professional duties without producing a false ethics score.

Map unequal exposure

Look for people who carry consequences but have little influence over the choice.

Separate reasoning types

Keep observations, evidence, assumptions, testimony, and professional judgment visibly distinct.

Keep the decision revisable

Baselines, monitoring, and review triggers keep approval from becoming permanent by inertia.
